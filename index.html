iThe big picture

No single wall stops everything. Defence in depth.

Real networks layer their defences. If one layer fails, the next catches what slipped through. Today's content sits in three concentric rings.

An attacker only has to succeed once — a defender has to succeed every time. That asymmetry is why we layer.

Teacher notes
3 mins · Q&A · Concept anchor

Ask: "Which ring stops phishing?" — innermost (training the human). Use to underline that security is not just technical. Return to this diagram visually at each transition.

01Outer ring

Firewalls.

A firewall sits at the boundary between trusted (your network) and untrusted (the internet). Its job is brutally simple: inspect every packet, apply the rules, allow or drop.

Packet filtering

The firewall inspects each packet's header against a rules table — checking source IP, destination IP, port number, and protocol. It does not look inside the payload. Fast, but stateless: each packet is judged in isolation.

Stateful inspection

A more sophisticated firewall tracks active connections. It remembers that you sent a request to a web server, so the response packet coming back is expected. An unsolicited packet pretending to be a "reply" is dropped.

Proxy firewall

The line between firewall and proxy blurs. A proxy firewall terminates the connection on one side, inspects everything (including application-layer content), and opens a new connection on the other side. We will meet proxies properly in the next section.

So what is the port number really for?

One computer runs many services at once — a web server, an email server, an SSH service, perhaps a database. They all share the same IP address. The destination port number in the TCP header is how the receiving computer decides which service should be handed the incoming data.

Yes — that is the correct interpretation. The Transport layer (TCP or UDP) writes both a source port and a destination port into its header. The destination port is the one that names the service to use; the source port lets the receiver send a reply back to the right process on the sender.

Why this matters for the firewall. A packet-filtering firewall reads the headers added by the Network layer (IP) and the Transport layer (TCP) — destination IP, destination port, protocol — and decides allow or deny on that alone. It does not open the application data. That is exactly why the firewall console above checked Source IP, Destination Port and Protocol: those are the fields visible in the outer headers.

02Outer ring

Proxies.

A proxy is a middle-man — it makes requests on your behalf. The destination server never talks to you directly. This unlocks four powerful behaviours: caching, filtering, anonymity, and logging.

Direct mode. The client and server communicate directly. The server logs the client's real IP. No caching, no filtering, no privacy. Toggle With proxy to see what changes.
Teacher notes
7 mins · Demo + Q&A

Run the animation twice — once direct, once with proxy. Ask: "In school, why does our network use a proxy?" Most will guess "to block YouTube" — affirm and extend: content filtering, caching for high-traffic pages, and logging for safeguarding.

Distinguish: a forward proxy represents the client (school's web proxy). A reverse proxy sits in front of servers (load balancer / CDN). H446 cares mostly about forward proxies, but knowing both impresses an examiner.

PPredict

Encryption I — the Caesar cipher.

Encryption transforms readable plaintext into unreadable ciphertext using an algorithm and a key. Caesar's cipher — Julius Caesar's actual war-time encryption — shifts each letter forward by a fixed number. The same key encrypts and decrypts: this is symmetric encryption.

Read the code below carefully. Before you press any buttons, work out the answer in your head and write your prediction. The rule: each letter is shifted forward by 3. A → D, B → E, C → F … X → A, Y → B, Z → C. Spaces stay as spaces.

IInvestigate

Investigate the cipher.

Think carefully about each question. Use the hint if you get stuck, then check the answer.

Q1. Why does "MEET ME" become "PHHW PH" and not "PHHWPHE" all run together?

Hint: The cipher only shifts letters of the alphabet.

Answer: Spaces are not in the alphabet, so they are not shifted — they stay as spaces and preserve the word boundaries.

Q2. If the shift is 3, what does X become? Why?

Hint: What happens when you fall off the end of the alphabet?

Answer: X → A. The alphabet wraps around. Mathematically we say the shift is performed modulo 26 — after Z you start again at A.

Q3. Is the Caesar cipher symmetric or asymmetric? How can you tell?

Hint: Count the keys.

Answer: Symmetric. The same key (the shift) is used to encrypt and to decrypt — you just shift the other way.

Q4. If Alice wants to send a Caesar-encrypted message to Bob, what serious problem do they have before they ever send a message?

Hint: What do they both need? How do they get it?

Answer: Both need the same key. They have to share the key safely. This is called the key-distribution problem and it is the central weakness of all symmetric encryption.

Q5. Why is the Caesar cipher trivial to break compared to modern AES?

Hint: How many possible keys are there?

Answer: Caesar has only 25 possible non-trivial shifts — try all 25 by brute force in under a second on any laptop. AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) is the modern symmetric cipher that replaced earlier standards: published by the US National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in 2001, it uses 128- or 256-bit keys. AES-128 has 2128 ≈ 3.4 × 1038 possible keys; AES-256 has 2256 — astronomically many, well beyond brute force on any computer that exists. AES-256 is the standard the US government uses to protect TOP SECRET information, and it is built into HTTPS (the padlock in your browser), BitLocker, FileVault, WhatsApp, Signal and most modern secure systems. Note: AES-256 is legal to use — strong encryption is not a crime. Some countries (China, Russia) restrict commercial cryptography products, and historically the US treated strong crypto as an export-controlled "munition" until around 2000 (the "Crypto Wars"), but ordinary use of AES is unrestricted in the UK and almost everywhere else.

Teacher notes

Have students write a 4-word message and a shift 1–25, swap with a partner, decrypt. Surfaces the key-distribution problem viscerally — they had to show the partner the key.

Real-world tie: Caesar is a toy. In production, symmetric means AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) with 128/256-bit keys, used by HTTPS, BitLocker, and Wi-Fi (WPA2/3).

That is the whole point of a cipher: change the key and the ciphertext changes completely, even though anyone who knows the algorithm cannot read the message without the key.

Teacher notes
12 mins · Direct instruction + demo

Use the padlock metaphor on the board: Alice's public key is a copy of her padlock, mass-produced and given to everyone. Anyone can close the padlock around a box. Only Alice's private key opens it. Exam-friendly and resilient.

Common misconception: students think both keys can encrypt and decrypt symmetrically. They can't. Public encrypts, private decrypts (for confidentiality). The reverse (private encrypts, public decrypts) is digital signatures — mention if time.

Examiner watchpoint: the comparison table is the most common 4–6 mark question shape in 1.3.3. Drill the row labels — students who name speed, key distribution, use case as their three axes always score full marks.

    05Inner ring · human & policy

    The human & policy layer.

    Technology alone won't save you. The most expensive firewall in the world fails the moment someone tapes their password to the monitor. The OCR spec is explicit: usage policy, secure passwords, training, anti-virus, backup and recovery.

    AV

    Anti-malware

    Scans files against a database of known malware signatures, plus heuristic behavioural analysis for unknown threats. Quarantines suspects; updates definitions daily.

    Counters · viruses · trojans · spyware
    BK

    Backups & recovery

    The 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media, 1 off-site. Combine full and incremental backups. Crucial against ransomware — pay nothing, just restore.

    Counters · ransomware · hardware failure
    PW

    Strong passwords + MFA

    Length beats complexity. Enforce minimum length, ban common words, require multi-factor authentication (something you know + something you have). Defeats most brute-force.

    Counters · brute force · credential stuffing
    UP

    Acceptable Use Policy

    A written contract: what users can and can't do on the network. No personal USBs, no installing software, no sharing credentials. Provides legal and procedural grounds for action.

    Counters · insider risk · shadow IT
    TR

    Staff & user training

    Phishing simulations, awareness sessions, secure-coding workshops. The single highest-ROI control — humans are the largest attack surface. Make it ongoing, not annual.

    Counters · phishing · social engineering
    LG

    Logging & monitoring

    You can't defend what you can't see. SIEM systems aggregate logs from firewalls, servers, and endpoints, and flag anomalies. Forensic value after the fact too.

    Counters · stealth intrusion · insider threat

    24 June 2024

    The Ladies' College · Guernsey

    How it happenedOn 24 June 2024 the school discovered it could no longer access several of its on-premises servers. Some data on those servers had been encrypted by attackers — the hallmark of a ransomware attack. The ODPA's subsequent investigation found that "failures in The Ladies' College's security allowed for unauthorised access to be gained to its systems".
    Why it happenedThe ODPA's report identified inadequate security measures. The good news: only limited examples of personal data were affected, no pupil data was accessed, and the encrypted data was recovered — suggesting working backups did their job. The College has since been ordered to improve its cyber security.

    Repercussions: ODPA findings published December 2025; remedial action ordered. Reputational impact for an island school whose families expect tight handling of personal information. No fine, because exposure was limited — but a public regulatory order is itself a significant signal.

    Maps to: backups & recovery (worked) · anti-malware & defence in depth (did not) · staff training.

    Medical Specialist Group LLP · Guernsey

    How it happenedThe MSG's email server was compromised in August 2021 through a collection of unpatched vulnerabilities. Attackers stole emails, some containing sensitive patient health data.     April–May 2025

    Marks & Spencer · UK attack with local impact

    How it happenedThe Scattered Spider collective social-engineered M&S's IT help desk, then deployed DragonForce ransomware. Online ordering, gift-card services and parts of the supply chain were taken offline. The same playbook hit Co-op and Harrods in the same window.
    Why it happenedStrong technical defences — but the human at the help desk was tricked into resetting credentials. The attackers exploited a process gap: how do you verify, by phone, that the caller is really who they claim to be?

    States of Guernsey · IT outage (Policy & Resources Committee)

    How it happenedBetween November 2022 and January 2023, several States of Guernsey IT systems went down — staff and members of the public could not use them or access the personal data they held. The cause was not a hacker: the air-conditioning system inside a States data room failed, taking servers and storage offline. Multiple technical and monitoring controls also failed at the same time.
    Why it happenedThe ODPA's October 2023 inquiry found that the Policy & Resources Committee (P&R) had failed to take reasonable steps to maintain the data-room air conditioning. More damning: P&R had also failed to implement an IT disaster-recovery plan before the incidents, so when things went wrong there was no rehearsed way to restore service.

    Repercussions: outages spanned two months, disrupting public-facing services that residents rely on. The ODPA issued a formal Reprimand after its inquiry, along with an action plan covering maintenance, monitoring and disaster recovery. P&R has since confirmed every recommendation is completed.

    ⚠Why this case still belongs here. Defence in depth is not only about attackers. A data room with no resilient cooling and no DR plan is a single point of failure — the effect on people is identical whether the cause is ransomware or a broken air-conditioning unit. Availability is part of security.

    Maps to: backups & recovery · disaster-recovery planning · monitoring & environmental controls · logging.

    MMake

    Design your own defence-in-depth.

    Now apply what you have learned. Read the scenario carefully and plan a layered defence.

    Scenario. A local independent bookshop has just launched an online store. Customer names, addresses and order history are stored on a small server in the back office, connected to the internet. The owner has a budget of about £2,000 a year for cyber security and one part-time IT volunteer.

    Recommend three prevention measures — one from each of the three rings (perimeter, data in transit, hosts & users). For each, briefly explain why you chose it for this business.

    This is for your own thinking — it is not marked. Bring your three to the board discussion.

    Example answer — one strong way to spend the £2,000. Yours may look different; defend your reasoning, not the wording.

    1 · Perimeter — a stateful firewall (~£150–250 one-off, ~£0/yr).
    Set up a stateful firewall on the boundary between the shop's network and the internet. Configure default-deny on inbound traffic; allow only TCP/443 (HTTPS) to the server. Explicitly deny ports 22 (SSH), 23 (Telnet) and 3389 (RDP) from the open internet. On a setup this size the firewall is normally built into the small-business router itself — no separate appliance needed.
    Why for this business: stops opportunistic scanning before it reaches the back-office server. A bookshop does not need a £5,000 enterprise firewall; the stateful firewall built into an SMB-grade router does the job. (A proxy is the lesson's other perimeter defence, but it is a better fit for organisations filtering outbound browsing — for a single-server bookshop, the firewall is the priority choice.)

    2 · Data in transit — HTTPS via a free TLS certificate (£0/yr).
    Install a free Let's Encrypt TLS certificate on the shop's website. Auto-renews every 90 days; modern browsers will display the padlock. Use TLS 1.3.
    Why for this business: protects customer names, addresses and order details from eavesdropping on the wire — public Wi-Fi sniffing, ISP-level snooping, man-in-the-middle. Free, standard, and the padlock visibly builds customer trust.

    3 · Hosts & users — daily off-site backups + free training (~£70–100/yr).
    Daily automated backups following the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies, 2 different media, 1 off-site (a cloud backup service such as Backblaze ~£70/yr). Pair with a 30-minute phishing-awareness session using the free NCSC Cyber Aware materials for the owner and the part-time volunteer.
    Why for this business: ransomware is the realistic biggest threat to a small UK business — see the Medical Specialist Group and Ladies' College cases above. Backups make ransomware survivable. The volunteer is the most likely entry point, so training delivers the highest return per pound spent.

    Budget left for stretch buys (well under £2,000): anti-malware licences (~£30/yr/device), free MFA via Microsoft or Google Authenticator on the admin console, and a one-page written Acceptable Use Policy for the volunteer (no personal USBs, no installing software, no sharing credentials — free to draft).

    Strong answers don't just pick three items — they justify each one for this business: small budget, single server, one volunteer, customer trust matters.

    Teacher notes
    5 mins · Independent · then cold call three

    Cold call one student per ring. Reward considered budget-aware choices (e.g. "a cheap router with a built-in firewall + free Let's Encrypt TLS + free MFA via Authenticator" beats "buy a £5k enterprise firewall" for this scenario).

    Excellent stretch answer: the part-time volunteer needs training first, because they are the single point of failure for everything else.

    ?Applying it · exam practice

    Exam-style question.

    A typical 1.3.3 question. Read it. Plan it. Then reveal the mark scheme one point at a time and self-check.

    H446/01 · Section B · Q-style [6 marks]

    A small business stores customer data on a server connected to the internet. Explain how a firewall, encryption, and one other prevention measure together protect this data.

    Suggested mark scheme

    [1]
    Firewall — function: filters inbound and outbound network traffic by inspecting packet headers against a set of rules.
    [2]
    Firewall — application: blocks unauthorised connections to the server (e.g. denying inbound traffic to admin ports such as 22 or 3389 from the open internet).
    [3]
    Encryption — function: converts plaintext into ciphertext using an algorithm and a key so that intercepted data is unreadable.
    [4]
    Encryption — application: protects customer data in transit (via TLS / HTTPS) so eavesdroppers cannot read it, and at rest via disk encryption so stolen drives are useless.
    [5]
    Third measure — function: e.g. regular backups store copies of data on separate media or off-site.
    [6]
    Third measure — application: if data is lost to ransomware or hardware failure, the business can restore from backups without paying a ransom or losing customer records.
    Revealed: 0 / 6
    Teacher notes
    8 mins · Independent writing → peer mark

    Routine: 4 minutes silent writing in books. Then reveal points one-by-one; students self-mark in another colour.

    The 6-mark "explain" template: for each measure named, give (a) what it does and (b) how it applies to this specific scenario. Two marks each, three measures = six marks. Make the structure explicit — pattern recognition is a teachable skill.

    Stretch: a student defending an unusual third measure (e.g. staff training — valid because phishing is how attackers usually get the password to reach the server). Reward answers that go beyond the obvious.

    💬Looking ahead · Discussion

    The next chapter — when AI joins both sides.

    In May 2026, Anthropic announced Claude Mythos Preview — its latest frontier AI model, strong enough at finding software flaws that Anthropic refused to release it publicly and instead launched Project Glasswing, sharing it only with around forty vetted organisations (Apple, Google, Microsoft, AWS, NVIDIA, JPMorgan, the Linux Foundation, and others) so they could patch first. The story behind every defence in this lesson is about to change. This section gives the class the questions to wrestle with it.

    What Mythos is — the short version

    • Who: Anthropic — the AI lab that makes Claude.
    • What: Anthropic's latest frontier model — with offensive cyber-security capability strong enough that, in early testing, it found thousands of high-severity, previously-unknown vulnerabilities — including in every major operating system and every major web browser. Some flaws were decades old.
    • How it was released: not publicly. Under Project Glasswing, Anthropic shared "Mythos Preview" with around 40 critical-infrastructure organisations so they could patch their software before anyone else could use Mythos-class models to attack them.
    • Why it matters here: what one frontier lab built in 2026, criminal groups and nation-states will race to build next. Every defence in this lesson now has a new opponent waiting.

    Five discussion questions

    There is no single right answer to any of these. Pair work for two minutes per question, then one answer per pair on the board.

    D1. If a frontier AI can find thousands of vulnerabilities in days, who does this favour in the long run — the attacker or the defender? Why?

    D2. Anthropic gave Mythos to 40 large organisations first (Apple, Google, JPMorgan, AWS, the Linux Foundation…) so they could patch before anyone else got Mythos-class tools. Was this responsible disclosure, or an unfair head start that leaves a Guernsey SME, the States of Guernsey, or your school further behind?

    D3. Look back at the five real-world cases above. If Mythos-class tools become cheaply available to criminals in two to three years, which single defence in this lesson would matter most to a Guernsey organisation — and which would matter least? Defend your ranking.

    D4. For an A Level student thinking about a career in cyber security: if AI can find flaws, write exploits, and draft patches — should you still learn the material in this lesson? What new skills become more valuable?

    D5. Bruce Schneier argues that AI "does not create new vulnerabilities — it exposes existing ones," and that the chronic under-investment in security was the real problem all along. Is Mythos a genuine inflection point in cyber, or is it a familiar threat re-packaged with new marketing?

    Teacher notes
    7 mins · Pair-and-share · Open debate

    Why this section exists. Cyber security in 2026 is in flux. A Level students considering this field need to think about it now, not after their exam. The questions above are deliberately open — there is no mark scheme, only the quality of reasoning.

    Bias to flag openly. Much of the Mythos narrative comes from Anthropic itself, which has a commercial interest in the story being significant. Lean hard into the skeptical sources (Schneier; the "Marketing Stunt" video) to keep the discussion balanced.     Stretch. Does the encapsulation visual from earlier change with AI in the loop? (Trick question — it doesn't; the layers and the destination port still mean what they meant. What changes is how fast attackers and defenders find new ways to abuse them.)

    Verify before delivery. The Mythos story is brand-new (May 2026). Re-check the linked articles on the day for any update or correction; details may shift.
